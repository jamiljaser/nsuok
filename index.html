Defining Differentiated Instruction

“In differentiated classrooms, teachers ensure that students compete against themselves as they grow and develop more than they compete against one another, always moving toward – and often beyond – designated content goals” (Tomlinson, 2014).

Move down to learn more about defining differentiation.

Differentiated classrooms are driven by a growth mindset.

Teachers differentiate content, process, product and environment, the curricular elements, according to students' individual readiness, interests, and learning profile, the three pillars of differentiation.

Curricular elements

Content pertains to the ideas a student will learn.

Process considers how students will take in information.

Product is how the student will be assessed.

The environment has to do with the tone of the classroom.

The Pillars of Differentiation

Readiness refers to the student's knowledge at the entry point compared to the knowledge required by the lesson

Interests are a motivational entry point, to begin dialogue with a student. They can be incorporated into lessons, and give clues about the types of intelligence a student may innately possess or that their interests would naturally require.

Learning profiles are composed of multiple datum that could include intelligence and learning preferences, as well as categorical info, such as gender.

“Differentiated instruction is not a single strategy, but rather an approach to instruction that incorporates a variety of strategies. In other words, differentiation is responsive instruction designed to meet unique individual student needs.” (Watts-Taffe, S. et al., 2012)

The instructor's role in differentiated instruction

"The teacher's key roles are to transmit to the student a belief in the student's ability to achieve (and often exceed) important learning goals, to mentor learners in working hard and working intelligently to achieve important learning goals, and to scaffold growth in learning in a stepwise fashion so that students most often (although not always) experience effort followed by success (Tomlinson, 2014)."

Tomlinson, C. A. (2014). The Differentiated Classroom: Responding to the Needs of All Learners (2nd ed.). ASCD

"Key roles for teachers, then, include both ensuring that students are appropriately challenged from their particular points of entry into a given topic of inquiry and helping students understand and become increasingly involved in the attitudes, practices, and habits of mind that contribute to positive brain development."

Tomlinson, C. A. (2014). The Differentiated Classroom: Responding to the Needs of All Learners (2nd ed.). ASCD

Most young learners don't know how to grow beyond where they are today, until a teacher shows the way (Tomlinson, 2014).

Two additional- and vital- teacher roles are discovering students' intellectual strengths and "assignment of status." Cohen (1994)

The teacher’s role becomes one of purposeful instruction, a mediator of activities and substantial experiences allowing the learner to attain his or her zone of proximal development (Blanton, 1998; Rueda et al., 1992).

Tomlinson (2014) describes teachers in healthy classrooms as teachers who invite students to participate in shaping the class.

"First, these teachers help students come to understand and contribute to the operation of a classroom that works for every student."

"Second, these teachers ensure that students know to teach one another and learn from one another effectively."

"Third, these teachers do "metacognitive teaching"; they explain to students- in ways that make sense to young learners- such things as how they plan for classes, what classroom issues they puzzle over when they go home at night, and how they chart progress."

The student's role in differentiated instruction

Students possess their own "tacit knowledge" that can inform the teacher about what works for them (Tomlinson, 2014)."

The student is the first, and primary source of information that a teacher needs in order to differentiate instruction. Their behaviors, and demonstrations are where to find out about their readiness, interests, and learning profile.

Move down to learn more about the student's role in differentiation.

Students can contribute to the class room eco-system by providing input on procedures.

Students can participate democratically with the teacher to determine how to create an equitable and effective learning environment for everyone in the class.

Students can contribute to each other, and gain from one another in peer teaching situations.

Students can reinforce procedures and lessons for their peers by modeling expected behaviors and recommunicating lesson directions

Students should learn that effort is how we improve our skills, and that the various innate abilities they and their peers possess are valuable.

Students also should learn to recognize and appreciate differences among their peers.

Evidence based practice in differentiation

Differentiated classrooms use assessments to inform the instructor about student needs. Teachers can modify instruction through content, process, product, and environment.

Move down to learn more about evidence based practice in differentiation.

Assessments are used in differentiation to get real-time, actionable data, or evidence, to customize a student's learning experience.

Evidence in differentiation comes from various forms of assessment.

Product, or the way a student will be assessed is one of the curricular elements of differentiation that a teacher can modify.

Varied means of assessment help students present their skills and comprehension.

Assessments inform how to modify the curricular elements of differentiation (content, process, product, and environment).

Some evidence can even be shared with students to help them better understand their progress.

"Encouraging students to analyze their own work relative to clearly articulated goals and criteria for success helps them consistently grow in independence, agency, and self-efficacy as learners (Tomlinson, 2014)."

Seven principles of differentiation

as outlined by Sousa and Tomlinson (2011)

These seven principles of differentiation will help create a succsessful learning environment with a growth mindset, which recognizes the diverse needs of students.

Move down to learn more about the seven principles of differentiation.

1) Every student in the classroom is important and worthy of respect. Recognizing that teaching is fundamentally about building lives (Tompkins, 1996), the teacher is consistently conscious of the value of each life and consistently seeks to dignify each student in individual interactions and in front of the group as a whole.

1) Example

2) A classroom environment that maximizes student potential reflects a high regard for the individuals in it, high challenge, and high support. Classroom environments that support the hard work and risk of learning begin with positive and growing connections between the teacher and each student, leading to a clear sense of team or community among the members of the class.
2) Example
3) Every student in the class should have access to a high-quality curriculum. The teacher’s job is to ensure that every student works with and masters essential knowledge, understanding, and skills at a level that enables the student to retain, apply and transfer what is essential in the content. Therefore, the teacher works to ensure that each student regularly engages with a curriculum that is inviting and that calls on the student to understand and reason.
3) Example
4) Students in a given class will differ in readiness, interest, and learning profile in ways that affect learning. These differences are influenced by a variety of factors and are both normal and valuable. Students learn best when teaching accounts for their various readiness levels, particular interests, and preferred ways of learning.
4) Example
5) The teacher’s job is to study the differences and to respond to them in ways that maximize student growth. Understanding students’ evolving learning needs is as central to a teacher’s success as understanding the content to be taught.
5) Example
6) The classroom belongs to everyone in it in terms of both opportunity and responsibility. Making the classroom work for everyone will require contributions from everyone.
6) Example
7) Teachers and students alike should have a shared vision of a classroom that works for everyone. As the architect of the vision, the teacher must lead the students in understanding, appreciating, refining, extending, implementing, and evaluating the vision.
7) Example

Rules of thumb for differentiation

Sousa and Tomlinson (2017)

Sousa and Tomlinson published six rules of thumb for successful differentiated instruction.

Move down to learn more about the rules of thumb for differentiation.

1) Know that highly effective teachers teach students first, then content.

Example

2) Be clear on the key concepts, generalizations, principles, or big ideas that give meaning and structure to the topic, curriculum, unit, lesson, or inquiry you are planning .


Having a clear idea of what is to be accomplished is a condition for success in any pursuit.

One effective way to portray a subject area is through a knowledge map. Dominic Walliman's knowledge maps are a popular example.

3) Think of assessment as a road map for your thinking and planning.

Data collection is common practice in modern business. Data is valuable to companies trying to make informed, high dollar design decisions. The stakes in the class room are arguably as significant as those faced by fortune 500 company's board of executives.

Assessment can provide a nearly real-time snapshot of the individual and immediate needs of your students. Assessment data can be plotted over time to track student progress.

4) Create lessons that engage all students in critical and creative thinking.

Adding value to educational content is purely in the domain of educators; and it can be attained by an instrument through its connection to the real world in meaning and effect (Harteveld, 2011).

5) Design lessons to be engaging for all students.

All forms of communication require some cognitive salience, or persuasive effort (pathos, logos, ethos) to be worth our attention, no matter our age.

6) Balance student-selected and teacher-assigned tasks and working arrangements

A motivational learning factor from Watt & Smith (2020) states that educational content sould allow for freedom of decision to promote a student's sense of self-determinism.

Giving students opportunities for choice can increase meaning in their learning journey. Students need opportunities to make decisions in a safe place in order to learn who they are, and who they want to become.

Watt & Smith (2020) also stated, as a social learning factor, that some level of guidance is of course needed from the teacher where discovery learning is in play.

Effective differentiated instruction

The effectiveness of differentiation is guided by a trifecta of philosophy, principles, and practices (Tomlinson, 2014)

Move down to learn more about the effectiveness of differentiation.

Philosophy

Philosophy contains ideas that set up the proper frame of mind or point of view to assume as a differentiating teacher.

Tomlinson (2014) identified four aspects of philosophy in differentiated instruction as follows:


Diversity is good, and to be expected.

Every student has an individual potential for academic success.

The role of the teacher is to accept the learner's progress as their mission, just like a coach, or trainer.

"Recognizing and removing barriers that deny many learners equal access to excellence"

Principles

Tomlinson's (2014) Principles of differentiation include the maintenance of a positive learning space suited to learning, the development of strong curricula, the use of assessment to inform instructional modifications, and leading a flexible learning space.

Practice

Tomlinson (2014) described practice as instructional modifications according to student readiness, interest, and learning profile.
