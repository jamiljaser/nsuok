Defining Differentiated Instruction

“In differentiated classrooms, teachers ensure that students compete against themselves as they grow and develop more than they compete against one another, always moving toward – and often beyond – designated content goals” (Tomlinson, 2014).

Move down to learn more about defining differentiation.

Differentiated classrooms are driven by a growth mindset.

Teachers differentiate content, process, product and environment, the curricular elements, according to students' individual readiness, interests, and learning profile, the three pillars of differentiation.

Curricular elements

Content pertains to the ideas a student will learn.

Process considers how students will take in information.

Product is how the student will be assessed.

The environment has to do with the tone of the classroom.

The Pillars of Differentiation

Readiness refers to the student's knowledge at the entry point compared to the knowledge required by the lesson

Interests are a motivational entry point, to begin dialogue with a student. They can be incorporated into lessons, and give clues about the types of intelligence a student may innately possess or that their interests would naturally require.

Learning profiles are composed of multiple datum that could include intelligence and learning preferences, as well as categorical info, such as gender.

“Differentiated instruction is not a single strategy, but rather an approach to instruction that incorporates a variety of strategies. In other words, differentiation is responsive instruction designed to meet unique individual student needs.” (Watts-Taffe, S. et al., 2012)

"The teacher's key roles are to transmit to the student a belief in the student's ability to achieve (and often exceed) important learning goals, to mentor learners in working hard and working intelligently to achieve important learning goals, and to scaffold growth in learning in a stepwise fashion so that students most often (although not always) experience effort followed by success (Tomlinson, 2014)."

Tomlinson, C. A. (2014). The Differentiated Classroom: Responding to the Needs of All Learners (2nd ed.). ASCD

Most young learners don't know how to grow beyond where they are today, until a teacher shows the way (Tomlinson, 2014).

Two additional- and vital- teacher roles are discovering students' intellectual strengths and "assignment of status." Cohen (1994)

The teacher’s role becomes one of purposeful instruction, a mediator of activities and substantial experiences allowing the learner to attain his or her zone of proximal development (Blanton, 1998; Rueda et al., 1992).

Tomlinson (2014) describes teachers in healthy classrooms as teachers who invite students to participate in shaping the class.

"First, these teachers help students come to understand and contribute to the operation of a classroom that works for every student."

"Second, these teachers ensure that students know to teach one another and learn from one another effectively."

"Third, these teachers do "metacognitive teaching"; they explain to students- in ways that make sense to young learners- such things as how they plan for classes, what classroom issues they puzzle over when they go home at night, and how they chart progress."

Advice on differentiation

as outlined by Sousa and Tomlinson (2011)

These seven principles of differentiation will help create a succsessful learning environment with a growth mindset, which recognizes the diverse needs of students.

Move down to learn more about the seven principles of differentiation.

1) Every student in the classroom is important and worthy of respect. Recognizing that teaching is fundamentally about building lives (Tompkins, 1996), the teacher is consistently conscious of the value of each life and consistently seeks to dignify each student in individual interactions and in front of the group as a whole.

1) Example

2) A classroom environment that maximizes student potential reflects a high regard for the individuals in it, high challenge, and high support. Classroom environments that support the hard work and risk of learning begin with positive and growing connections between the teacher and each student, leading to a clear sense of team or community among the members of the class.
2) Example
3) Every student in the class should have access to a high-quality curriculum. The teacher’s job is to ensure that every student works with and masters essential knowledge, understanding, and skills at a level that enables the student to retain, apply and transfer what is essential in the content. Therefore, the teacher works to ensure that each student regularly engages with a curriculum that is inviting and that calls on the student to understand and reason.
3) Example
4) Students in a given class will differ in readiness, interest, and learning profile in ways that affect learning. These differences are influenced by a variety of factors and are both normal and valuable. Students learn best when teaching accounts for their various readiness levels, particular interests, and preferred ways of learning.
4) Example
5) The teacher’s job is to study the differences and to respond to them in ways that maximize student growth. Understanding students’ evolving learning needs is as central to a teacher’s success as understanding the content to be taught.
5) Example
6) The classroom belongs to everyone in it in terms of both opportunity and responsibility. Making the classroom work for everyone will require contributions from everyone.
6) Example
7) Teachers and students alike should have a shared vision of a classroom that works for everyone. As the architect of the vision, the teacher must lead the students in understanding, appreciating, refining, extending, implementing, and evaluating the vision.
7) Example
